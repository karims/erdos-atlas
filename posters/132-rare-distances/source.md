## Problem ID
132

## Source link
https://www.erdosproblems.com/132

## Exact statement

Let $A\subset \mathbb{R}^2$ be a set of $n$ points. Must there be two distances which occur at least once but between at most $n$ pairs of points? Must the number of such distances $\to \infty$ as $n\to \infty$?

## Short label
Rare distances

## Status
Open

## Prize
$100

## Tags
distances

## Notes

The page notes that Hopf and Pannowitz proved that the largest distance between points of \(A\) can occur at most \(n\) times.

However, it is unknown whether a second such distance must occur.

The page also notes that Erdős believed that for \(n\geq 5\), there must always exist at least two such distances, but this is false for \(n=4\).

## Visual metaphor

A finite constellation of points determines many pairwise distances.  
Some lengths appear over and over, while rare lengths appear only a few times.  
The problem asks whether at least two rare lengths must always exist, and whether their number grows without bound.
